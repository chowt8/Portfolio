Helping people find more things to do locally and while traveling on Yelp

Year

2019

Team

Marketplaces

Role

Product Designer

Platform

iOS

Background

During the first few months of 2019 I worked closely with a product manager on a expansion proposal into the vast space of Things To Do (TTD) on Yelp. Our goal was to create new avenues for people to find:

  • Sightseeing tours
  • Tourist attractions
  • National parks and monuments
  • Outdoor recreation, hikes and trails
  • Museums and art venues
  • Anything with a ticket purchase
  • Anything with an in-advance reservation
  • Local events listed on Yelp

Coupling a discovery experience with an intergated booking feature would provide people a way to secure tickets or reservations directly from Yelp's app or website without being pushed to a 3rd party experience.

Overview

There were two large phases to this initiative. In Phase 1., I was asked to quickly design an experience that integrates a ticket booking partner into Yelp's iOS app.
In Phase 2., a product manager and I conducted a series of remote user interviews, examined available search data, and organized a generative design workshop to visualize how a TTD discovery experience could co-exist with everything else Yelp has to offer.

My role and the team

I owned all of the design work during this initiative from low to high fidelity. In addition to the final visuals I also did competitive research, wrote our research interview guide, conducted remote user interviews, handpicked all the photography, and wrote all the copy. I worked in very close partnership with one product manager on this effort for over two months.

Phase 1. The partner integration

Searching for things to do

Having previously led design on our revamped search results and homescreen, I showcased how our top of funnel could easily build awareness of things to do categories and surface unique offerings and deals. I even went as far to sketch out some things to do specific icons to grow our illustration library.

Two is greater than one

Something we realized that was unique about things to do categories is that points of interest, like Alcatraz Island, and businesses that provide tours to Alcatraz, like Red & White Fleet, both have a presence on Yelp as a "business" page. This makes it easier for people to find available inventory and book it whether they go to the point of interest page first or directly to the service provider's business page on Yelp.

Using our existing paradigms

I used Yelp's Grubhub integration as a model to build off of and set constraints to stick within our existing library of components, patterns and screens. This was to ensure our first MVP would be realistically scoped.

As I designed, I found that our food delivery menus naturally lent themselves to hosting a provider's service offerings, while our newly designed native checkout experience could facilitate the booking and purchase.

Phase 2. Going broad

The big question

While the partner integration was being assessed and scoped out, my partner and I began tackling the research. The main question we wanted to answer was which use case has broader appeal from existing Yelp users: Surfacing things to do locally (where someone lives) or things to do while traveling (planned vacations / destinations).

What we heard

We spoke to 6 people remotely for one hour each to get a sense of their unique perspectives when it comes to finding and committing to things to do locally and while traveling. Some interesting things we heard in regards to the travel usecase:

“I’m not the expert of Austin, Texas. Someone that has been there multiple times can tell me different things that I’ve never seen, so we’ll watch a bunch of videos and come up with a short list of things we'd like to do to plan out our days accordingly.”

– Remote research participant #1, 2019

On [Pinterest] you can search 3 days in wherever city and a whole bunch of stuff will pop up. Like, I search for: 10 things you can’t miss in Ireland and I get almost a prebuilt trip”

– Remote research participant #2, 2019

The one stop shop

Finally one common thread we picked up on in local and traveling things to do use cases was the idea of "one stop shopping". People want to be able to discovery and book all from the same place.

“[Google] is sort of one stop shopping...I can get all the information I need to make a decision what I want to see when and where.”

– Remote research participant #3, 2019

“I’ve used Roadtrippers to plan a long trip, I printed out all the suggestions from the trip. It made it easier to plan where we’ll kill a couple hours at before moving on to the next place.”

– Remote research participant #4, 2019

Design workshop

Ideas we liked and next steps

We landed on two ideas that we thought were realistic enough to puruse. The first one was appropriating our city pages that are only available on the Yelp website and location focused discover pages. They would weave together city highlights, events and all things that are conveiably "things to do".

These discovery pages would exist as a middle layer between the homepage and search results resulting from a low-intent search.

A grab-bag of things I designed and explored during the project.

The other idea that drew directly from what we heard during our remote user sessions was creating itneraries with swappable steps and "one stop shopping" booking buttons. We felt this could live in isolation but potentially also expand upon our existing collections tab.

Takeaways

I see multiple successes in this project. Our phased approach enabled my partner and I to cover a lot of ground. By starting with the integration we were able to pressure test our transaction paradigms from food delivery into this new use case. Finally, the research and design workshop highlighted unique ways to weave in existing city pages (from web) and events on Yelp which are otherwise not prominently featured anywhere else.
